# Design Principles

## Universal AI Symbol Project

**Version:** 1.0

**Status:** Approved

**Created:** 2026

---

# Introduction

A universal symbol should not be evaluated solely by personal preference.

Instead, it should be assessed according to principles that encourage clarity, longevity, accessibility, and openness.

These principles provide a common framework for evaluating both the initial symbol proposal and any future alternatives.

They are intended to guide discussion rather than restrict creativity.

---

# Principle 1 — Simplicity

A symbol should be visually simple.

It should avoid unnecessary details while remaining distinctive.

The simpler a symbol is, the easier it becomes to recognize, remember, and reproduce.

---

# Principle 2 — Drawability

A person should be able to draw the symbol by hand without specialized tools.

A symbol that can be reproduced from memory is more likely to become part of everyday communication.

---

# Principle 3 — Scalability

The symbol should remain recognizable at both very small and very large sizes.

Its identity should not depend on fine details that disappear when reduced.

---

# Principle 4 — Neutrality

The symbol should represent the concept of Artificial Intelligence rather than any specific implementation.

It should avoid direct associations with robots, computer chips, brains, companies, or products.

---

# Principle 5 — Timelessness

The design should remain meaningful across decades.

It should avoid trends that may become visually outdated.

---

# Principle 6 — Memorability

After a brief exposure, people should be able to recognize the symbol again.

Recognition is one of the defining characteristics of successful symbols.

---

# Principle 7 — Reproducibility

The symbol should work consistently across different media, including print, engraving, embroidery, digital interfaces, three-dimensional objects, and physical signage.

---

# Principle 8 — Animatability

The symbol should naturally support animation without requiring additional decorative elements.

Movement should emerge from its own geometry.

---

# Principle 9 — Universality

The symbol should be understandable regardless of language, writing system, or cultural background.

Its meaning should not depend on textual explanations.

---

# Principle 10 — Openness

The design should remain freely available for use, modification, distribution, and implementation by anyone.

Its adoption should always be voluntary.

---

# Conclusion

A symbol should not be considered successful because it is visually attractive alone.

Its success depends on how well it fulfills the principles that allow people to understand, remember, reproduce, and voluntarily adopt it over time.
